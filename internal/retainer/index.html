Retainer Structure

This page captures the default Benefactor model for retainers: agency fees billed in advance, clear scope boundaries, and separate handling for pass-through spend such as ad budget, software, or third-party tools.

Default Model

How Benefactor should get paid on retainer

For recurring engagements, the cleanest setup is a monthly retainer billed in advance for Benefactor's labor, strategy, execution, reporting, and operating cadence. That retainer is separate from any spend that flows to ad platforms, tools, or third-party vendors.

Budget LineWhat It CoversHow It Should Work
Agency RetainerStrategy, campaign management, landing pages, reporting, meetings, optimization, and internal execution.Billed monthly in advance, due before the service month begins.
Pass-Through Ad SpendGoogle Ads, Meta, LinkedIn, programmatic, or other direct media costs.Should remain separate from the retainer and tied to a client-owned or client-controlled funding method.
Third-Party ToolsCreative tools, call tracking, landing page software, data enrichers, and platform subscriptions.Either billed directly to the client or approved as reimbursable with a documented ceiling.
Scope Control

What the retainer should explicitly define

Commercial guardrails

  • Monthly retainer amount and billing date
  • Included channels, deliverables, and cadence
  • Approval threshold for out-of-scope work
  • Who owns ad accounts, data, creatives, and landing pages
  • What happens if spend needs to increase mid-cycle

Operating guardrails

  • Reporting cadence, usually weekly or biweekly with a monthly review
  • Named approvers on the client side
  • Expected response windows for launch approvals or creative signoff
  • What counts as urgent work vs scheduled optimization
  • What happens if approvals delay media execution
Recommended Position

Preferred retainer stance for Benefactor

Preferred structure: Benefactor charges a monthly retainer for the work, while the client separately funds media spend and third-party platform costs. That keeps agency revenue clean, avoids muddling labor fees with pass-through spend, and gives both sides clearer reporting.

Why this is cleaner

  • It simplifies accounting and tax treatment.
  • It avoids the agency accidentally financing media with its own cash flow.
  • It gives the client visibility into actual media and platform costs.
  • It makes overages and approvals easier to govern.

What to avoid

  • Bundling large ad budgets into the retainer without separate controls.
  • Letting reimbursable spend accumulate without a written ceiling.
  • Fronting large media budgets unless there is a clear commercial reason.
  • Using vague language about "miscellaneous marketing costs."
